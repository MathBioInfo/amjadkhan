Research

Modern technological advancements have resulted in an avalanche of data on a wide range of biological phenomena. These findings urge mathematical modellers to use complementary approaches from mathematical modelling and bioinformatics to tackle a wide range of problems. The best use of this data in mathematical modelling of these problems will result in more accurate models and aid in the extraction of more information about the solutions to these challenges.

My research focuses on mathematical modelling of biological and health-related problems, notably the evolution of bacterial genomes.The emergence and rapid spread of antimicrobial resistance (AMR) genes is a great concern, as widely used antibiotics are becoming less effective in the treatment of infectious diseases. Mobile genetic elements (MGEs), in the bacterial genomes, play a critical role in the spread of these AMR genes. Mobile genetic elements include plasmids, prophages, and integrative conjugative elements (ICEs), etc. Although extensively studied, the magnitude of their infuence remains unclear, and more mathematical modeling is essential to understanding their history and the future risks they pose. I am employing mathematical modeling and bioinformatics techniques to study the evolution and gene repertoire of MGEs. I have also worked on Fermi-Pasta-Ulam lattice dynamics and reaction-diffusion equations.

Currently, I am a Postdoctoral Fellow in Dr. Robert Beiko's Bioinformatics group at the Faculty of Computer Science at Dalhousie University, where I am working on the Genome Canada-funded project Antimicrobial Resistance: Emergence, Transmission, and Ecology (ARETE). In collaboration with experts from the Public Health Agency of Canada and Agriculture/Agri-Food Canada, I am building mathematical models of antibiotic resistance spread in clinical, agricultural, and environmental settings.
