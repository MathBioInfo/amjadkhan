Research

My research interests lie in the mathematical modeling of biological and health-related problems with an emphasis on bacterial pathogen evolution and infectious diseases. The development of modern technologies has resulted in a huge amount of data about various biological phenomena. The maximum utilization of this available data in mathematical modeling of these problems will result in more informed models and help in extracting more information. These data invite the mathematical modeler to use complementary approaches between mathematical modeling and bioinformatics to answer different questions of interest. In my Ph.D. research, in addition to predictive modeling, the emphasis was on the use of available data to build more accurate and informed models. To further enhance these skills, in my postdoctoral study under the supervision of Robert Beiko (Dalhousie University), I am learning about different bioinformatics tools to generate new data and extract information from the available data to develop more informed mathematical models.

The emergence and rapid spread of antimicrobial resistance (AMR) genes is a great concern, as widely used antibiotics are becoming less effective in the treatment of infectious diseases. Mobile genetic elements (MGEs), in the bacterial genomes, play a critical role in the spread of these AMR genes. Mobile genetic elements include plasmids, prophages, and integrative conjugative elements (ICEs), etc. Although extensively studied, the magnitude of their infuence remains unclear, and more mathematical modeling is essential to understanding their history and the future risks they pose. I have employed skills that combine mathematical modeling and bioinformatics to study the evolution and gene repertoire of prophages. In addition to these approaches, I have also worked on Fermi-Pasta-Ulam lattice dynamics and reaction-diffusion equations.
